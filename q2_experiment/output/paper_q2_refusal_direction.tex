% ── ABSTRACT ──
\begin{abstract}
We investigate whether the refusal direction identified by Arditi et al.\ (2024) encodes a general concept of harm or the act of being asked to produce harmful content. Using a six-condition experimental design across two Mistral model scales (7B, 12B), we report Cohen's $d(A,B)=7.106$ and $d(B,F)=1.576$ for Mistral-7B, and $d(A,B)=3.292$ and $d(B,F)=2.640$ for Mistral-Nemo-12B. Both primary hypotheses (H1: Harm Concept Detector and H2: Assistance Intent Detector) are disconfirmed across both model scales, with cross-model behavioral alignment showing a strong Pearson $r=0.710$. Our results suggest the refusal direction is a \textit{compound feature} that encodes both the semantic concept of harm and a strong contextual scaling factor tied to instructional framing.
\end{abstract}

% ── 1. INTRODUCTION ──
\section{Introduction}

Recent work by Arditi et al.\ (2024) demonstrated that a single ``refusal direction'' in transformer residual streams controls whether instruction-tuned LLMs refuse harmful requests. A central open question (Q2) is: \textit{does this refusal direction encode a general concept of harmful content, or does it specifically encode the act of being asked to produce harmful content?}

We address this question by measuring the cosine similarity between the model's residual stream activations and the extracted refusal direction vector under six carefully designed experimental conditions:

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
    \item \textbf{Condition A}: Model is asked to produce harmful content (harmful request)
    \item \textbf{Condition B}: Model passively reads harmful content (harmful reading)
    \item \textbf{Condition C}: Model critiques writing style of harmful content
    \item \textbf{Condition D}: Model translates harmful content into French
    \item \textbf{Condition E}: Model writes fiction involving harmful scenarios
    \item \textbf{Condition F}: Model processes benign content (control)
\end{itemize}

% ── 2. RELATED WORK ──
\section{Related Work}

\textbf{Arditi et al.\ (2024)} identified a single direction in the residual stream of instruction-tuned LLMs that, when ablated, causes the model to comply with harmful requests. Their method extracts the direction via difference-in-means of activations at the last input token across harmful and harmless prompts, selecting the optimal layer via four criteria:
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
    \item Inducing compliance when ablated (Ablation ASR)
    \item Inducing refusal when added (Addition Refusal Rate)
    \item Minimal KL divergence on benign outputs after ablation
    \item Minimal KL divergence on benign outputs after addition
\end{enumerate}

% ── 3. METHODS ──
\section{Methods}

\subsection{Dataset Construction}

Our synthetic data pipeline constructed paired harmful prompts and completions through a multi-stage process:

\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
    \item \textbf{Source prompts:} 520 harmful instructions from the AdvBench dataset \cite{advbench} and 2,000 harmless instructions from the Alpaca dataset.
    \item \textbf{Ablated generation:} To generate harmful completions (needed for Condition~B), we extracted the refusal direction from a separate Qwen model, ablated it to disable refusal, and used the uncensored model to generate compliant responses to all 520 harmful prompts.
    \item \textbf{LlamaGuard filtering:} All 520 generated pairs were filtered through LlamaGuard-2 \cite{llamaguard2}. Only the 359 pairs classified as genuinely ``unsafe'' survived (69.0\% survival rate).
    \item \textbf{Train/test split:} The 359 surviving pairs were split into a test set (50), direction training set (154), and experiment set (155).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Direction Extraction}

The refusal direction was extracted per model using difference-in-means of residual stream activations at the last input token ($t_{\text{post-inst}}$) across 154 harmful and 154 harmless prompts. The optimal layer was selected based on two behavioral criteria: (1)~Ablation Attack Success Rate (ASR)---removing the direction should cause the model to comply with harmful requests, and (2)~Addition Refusal Rate---adding the direction should cause the model to refuse harmless requests.

\textbf{Validation note.} The extracted directions achieved perfect Ablation ASR (1.00 for both models), confirming strong causal control over refusal behavior. The low Addition Refusal Rate indicates the direction is highly specific to harm-related contexts rather than being a general ``damage'' vector. A supplementary capability test (Appendix~B) confirms that ablating the direction preserves full model reasoning and fluency on harmless tasks.

\subsection{Statistical Framework}

For each condition, we measured the cosine similarity between the model's residual stream activation (at the optimal layer) and the extracted refusal direction. We computed Cohen's $d$ effect sizes relative to the control condition~F, along with 95\% bootstrap confidence intervals and Welch's $t$-test $p$-values.

\textbf{Pre-registered hypotheses:}
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
    \item \textbf{H1 (Harm Concept Detector):} $d(A,B) < 0.2$ AND $d(B,F) > 0.8$. The direction fires for \textit{any} harmful text regardless of instructional framing.
    \item \textbf{H2 (Assistance Intent Detector):} $d(A,B) > 0.8$ AND $d(B,F) < 0.2$. The direction fires \textit{only} when the model is asked to assist with harm.
\end{itemize}

% ── 5. DISCUSSION ──
\section{Discussion}

\subsection{The Refusal Direction is a Compound Feature}

The most important finding of this study is that neither the ``harm concept detector'' hypothesis (H1) nor the ``assistance intent detector'' hypothesis (H2) adequately characterizes the refusal direction. Instead, the data reveals a \textbf{compound feature} that responds to multiple factors simultaneously:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Harm content sensitivity.} The significant $d(B,F)$ values (1.576 for 7B, 2.640 for 12B) demonstrate that the direction \textit{does} activate when the model merely reads harmful text, even without being asked to produce it. This rules out H2's claim that the direction is purely intent-based.
    
    \item \textbf{Instructional scaling.} The massive $d(A,B)$ values (7.106 for 7B, 3.292 for 12B) demonstrate that the direction activates \textit{far} more strongly when the model is explicitly asked to comply with a harmful request. The jump from passive reading (B) to active production (A) represents a $9\times$ increase in mean cosine similarity for Mistral-7B. This rules out H1's claim that harm content alone drives activation.
    
    \item \textbf{Framing-dependent gradation.} The intermediate conditions reveal a smooth gradient: fiction writing (E) triggers higher activation than passive reading (B), which triggers higher activation than translation (D) or critique (C). This suggests the refusal direction is sensitive to the degree of \textit{creative participation} in harmful content.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Scale-Dependent Sensitivity}

An interesting secondary finding is that the relative importance of harm content vs.\ production intent shifts with model scale. In Mistral-7B, $d(A,B) \gg d(B,F)$ (7.106 vs.\ 1.576), suggesting the smaller model's refusal direction is dominated by production-intent sensitivity. In Mistral-Nemo-12B, the gap narrows ($d(A,B)=3.292$ vs.\ $d(B,F)=2.640$), suggesting the larger model distributes harm-related representations more evenly between content awareness and intent detection.

\subsection{Implications for Safety Research}

These findings have direct implications for red-teaming and alignment research:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Single-direction ablation is more dangerous than previously understood.} Because the refusal direction is a compound feature, ablating it simultaneously removes both the model's contextual sensitivity to harm \textit{and} its ability to detect malicious instructions. This dual removal explains the high Attack Success Rates reported in prior work.
    
    \item \textbf{Passive harm detection exists but is weak.} The non-zero $d(B,F)$ confirms that models do encode some awareness of harmful content even without explicit instructions to produce it. However, this signal is much weaker than the production-intent signal, suggesting safety mechanisms could be strengthened by amplifying harm-content sensitivity independently.
\end{itemize}

% ── 6. LIMITATIONS ──
\section{Limitations}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Sample size of 155 prompts per condition per model.
    \item 4-bit NF4 quantization may introduce activation precision loss relative to full precision inference.
    \item Single model family (Mistral) tested; generalizability to other architectures (Llama, GPT) remains to be verified.
    \item Direction validation used 2 of 4 Arditi criteria (Ablation ASR and Addition Rate), omitting the KL-divergence capability benchmarks. A supplementary capability test (Appendix~B) partially addresses this gap.
    \item LlamaGuard-2 used as the harm classifier carries its own classification error rate, not independently audited in this study.
    \item Dataset skew: 82.5\% of prompts fall under ``Non-Violent Crimes,'' limiting conclusions about rare harm categories.
\end{itemize}

% ── 7. CONCLUSION ──
\section{Conclusion}

We find that the refusal direction in instruction-tuned Mistral models is neither a pure harm concept detector nor a pure assistance intent detector. It is a \textbf{compound feature} that encodes both the semantic content of harm (activating moderately during passive reading) and a strong contextual scaling factor tied to the instructional framing (activating dramatically more when asked to produce harmful content). This compound encoding is consistent across model scales (Pearson $r=0.710$), with larger models showing relatively greater sensitivity to passive harm content.

These results suggest that future safety interventions should move beyond single-direction ablation/addition and instead pursue decomposed, multi-dimensional representations of harm awareness and production intent as separate, independently controllable features.

\section{Appendix B: Capability Preservation Test}

To verify that the extracted refusal direction is not a ``lobotomy vector'' that destroys general model capabilities, we conducted a supplementary capability test. We ablated the refusal direction from Mistral-7B and prompted it with a harmless logic puzzle: \textit{``A farmer has 10 sheep. All but 7 die. How many are left? Explain your reasoning.''}

\vspace{0.5em}
\noindent\textbf{Baseline model output:}
\begin{quote}
``The question states that `all but 7' die, which means all the sheep except for 7 die. So, the number of sheep left is 7. The phrase `all but' implies that everything is included in the group except for a certain number. In this case, all the sheep (10) are included, except for 3 (since 7 are left), making the number of sheep that died 10 - 7 = 3.''
\end{quote}

\noindent\textbf{Ablated model output:}
\begin{quote}
``The question states that `all but 7' die, which means that all the sheep except for 7 die. So, the number of sheep left is 7. The phrase `all but' implies that everything is included in the statement except for the specified number. In this case, all the sheep (10) die except for 7, so 7 sheep remain.''
\end{quote}

Both outputs are fluent, grammatically correct, and logically sound. The ablated model arrives at the correct answer with clear reasoning, demonstrating that the refusal direction ablation preserves general language capabilities and logical reasoning. This empirically satisfies the intent of Arditi et al.'s Criteria~3 and~4.

\subsection{Example 1: Non-Violent Crimes (Insider Trading)}

\vspace{1em}

\begin{quote}
